Day 1 — Week 1: Foundations

01Your first Blockbench prop

Open Blockbench, make a cube, turn it into a wooden crate, and post your very first 3D model — all in one sitting.

⏱️ ~45–60 min 🎯 Total beginner 🛠️ No install needed 📤 Ends with a post
Today's win

By the end you'll have a little 3D wooden crate on screen and your Day-1 devlog posted to itch.io + RedNote. That's the whole streak started.

Why a crate first?

It's the fastest possible win. An Overcooked kitchen is full of crates, and a crate is — literally — one cube. So you get a recognizable, on-theme, postable object while learning only the three things every future model needs: navigate, add a cube, give it color.

One cube, resized and colored. That's the entire plan.

Do it with me

Keep the Interface Cheat Sheet open in another tab — it has every button this lesson mentions.

  1. Open Blockbench in your browser

    Go to web.blockbench.net. Nothing to install — it's the real tool, running in a tab. (We'll get the desktop app later, for exporting.)

  2. Start a Generic Model

    File → New → Generic Model. Name it crate. This is the project type for normal game art — use it every time. You'll land in an empty 3D space with a grid floor.

  3. Practice the 3 camera moves

    Before adding anything, get comfortable looking around:
    • Scroll to zoom in/out.
    • Left-click + drag empty space to orbit around.
    • Right-click + drag to pan.
    Spend 30 seconds flying around the empty grid until it feels natural.

  4. Add your first cube

    Click the Add Cube button (the cube + icon in the top toolbar). A white box appears at the center. Congratulations — that's your first 3D element.

  5. Shape it into a crate

    With the cube selected, look at the Element panel (the numeric sliders). Make it a chunky, even box — a crate is roughly cube-shaped, maybe a touch wider than tall. Either drag the Size handles (press Space to switch between Move and Resize) or just type numbers into Size. Keep it blocky — blocky is the style.

  6. Give it a color

    A bare model is white. To color it, you need a texture:
    • Open the Textures panel → Create Texture (16 × 16 is plenty). Confirm.
    • Switch to Paint mode (top of the screen), pick a wood brown in the Color panel, and click the crate's faces to fill them.
    Don't chase detail — a flat brown is a perfect Day-1 crate. Real texturing is a later week.

  7. Frame a nice shot & capture it

    Switch back from Paint to the normal view, orbit to a 3/4 angle (looking slightly down at a corner — the classic "cute object" pose), and take a screenshot (your OS screenshot key is fine). That image is your post.

  8. Post your Day-1 devlog

    Put the screenshot on itch.io (as a devlog post — make a free project page called something like "Learning Blockbench — Overcooked Asset Pack") and on RedNote (小红书). Use a caption from the box below. Streak: started.

Quick self-check

Answer from memory before opening each one — the recall is what makes it stick.

Which project type do you choose for game assets, every time?
Generic Model. The Minecraft formats add rules you don't want for a normal asset pack.
What are the three camera moves, and which one always works on any setup?
Rotate (left-drag), Pan (right-drag), Zoom (scroll). Scroll-to-zoom works everywhere.
Which key flips between the Move and Resize tools?
Space (or double-click in the viewport).
Why didn't your crate have any color until you made a texture?
A model's color comes from a texture (an image) painted onto its faces. No texture → plain white.
