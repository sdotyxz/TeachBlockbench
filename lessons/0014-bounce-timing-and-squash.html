14Bounce timing and squash - make the tomato feel heavier

Yesterday's tomato moved. Today it gets weight. You will keep the same tiny bounce loop, then add timing decisions and Scale keyframes so the motion feels more like a game asset and less like a sliding object.

Today's win

One improved 1-second tomato bounce GIF: the tomato squashes on impact, stretches during takeoff, hangs briefly at the top, and lands cleanly into the loop.

Lesson target: the model still stays simple, but the timing now explains weight.

Warm up - 30-second recall

Answer first, then open the cards.

What is a keyframe?
A saved pose or value at a specific time in the animation.
Why did the first and last bounce keyframes match?
Matching start and end keyframes let the loop restart without a visible jump.
Why did you animate the tomato group instead of every part?
One group keeps the whole tomato moving together and makes the Timeline easier to debug.

The one new idea - scale can fake weight

You do not need a character rig to show weight. In a small stylized prop, a quick squash and stretch can sell the impact: wide and short when the tomato hits the counter, tall and narrow when it springs upward, normal at the top.

Blockbench's official Overview & Tips page says Animate mode has a Keyframe Panel, an interpolation dropdown, and a Timeline for keyframes. The local source shows new keyframes default to linear interpolation, so today's motion stays predictable while you learn the Scale channel.

Beginner boundary

Skip Bezier handles and the Graph Editor today. They are real tools, but this lesson's win is smaller: clear time placement plus a few Scale keyframes.

Do it with me

  1. Open yesterday's bouncing tomato file

    Save a copy named bouncing-tomato-squash-loop. Keep yesterday's file intact so you can show a before/after if you want.

  2. Preview the old loop once

    In Animate mode, play the current tomato_bounce animation. Notice the problem: the tomato moves up and down, but it has little weight.

  3. Rename the animation

    Duplicate or rename the animation to tomato_squash_bounce. Keep the length at 1.0s.

  4. Use five timing points

    Keep the loop simple: 0.00s, 0.12s, 0.28s, 0.50s, and 1.00s. These are close enough for a tiny game-prop loop.

  5. Impact at 0.00s: squash

    Select tomato_bounce_group. At 0.00s, add or select a Scale keyframe. Try X 1.15, Y 0.85, Z 1.15.

  6. Recovery at 0.12s: normal shape

    Move to 0.12s. Add a Scale keyframe with X 1, Y 1, Z 1. This makes the squash snap back quickly.

  7. Takeoff at 0.28s: stretch

    Move to 0.28s. Add a Scale keyframe with X 0.92, Y 1.12, Z 0.92. This makes the upward motion feel springy.

  8. Top at 0.50s: normal shape and hang

    Move to 0.50s. Keep the tomato at the top position and set Scale back to 1, 1, 1. If the top feels too fast, hold the top position until about 0.58s with one extra Position keyframe.

  9. Landing at 1.00s: match the start

    Move to 1.00s. The tomato should be on the counter again with the same squash as 0.00s: X 1.15, Y 0.85, Z 1.15.

  10. Keep interpolation linear

    Select the transform keyframes and check the Keyframe Panel. Leave Interpolation on Linear. Today's shape changes are already enough.

  11. Preview at thumbnail size

    Turn on Looped Playback and zoom out. If the squash is invisible, increase it a little. If it looks like a bug, decrease it.

  12. Capture the improved loop

    Export or record a GIF with a fixed camera. Optional but useful: put yesterday's GIF beside today's GIF for a before/after post.

  13. Post your Day-14 update

    Publish the GIF and a short note. Streak: Day 14.
Animation habit

When a loop feels stiff, do not add complexity first. Ask: which keyframes say impact, which say hang, and which say recovery?

Quick self-check

Say the answer first, then open the card.

What does a Scale keyframe store?
It stores the selected group's size values at one time in the animation.
Why squash the tomato at impact?
The wide, short shape makes the landing feel heavier and less rigid.
Why keep the final Scale keyframe equal to the first?
Matching start and end scale values prevents a visible loop pop.
What should you do if the squash looks too silly?
Reduce the scale change and preview again at thumbnail size.
