Day 12 - Week 2: Pixel art fundamentals

12Pixel texture foundations - clusters, contrast, and the thumbnail test

You have painted your first texture, but pixel art itself is still new. Today you learn the smallest useful unit of pixel-art thinking for 3D model texturing: make readable material clusters with only three colors.

~45-60 min Builds on Lessons 5 and 6 Browser Blockbench is fine Ends with itch.io homework
Today's win

One pixel texture study applied to a Blockbench prop: a tiny wood-material swatch, a prop render that uses it, and a short itch.io progress post explaining what you learned.

Warm up - 30-second recall

Answer first, then open the cards.

Why did we make a shared palette in Lesson 5?
A shared palette makes separate props read as one asset pack instead of unrelated practice models.
What did Lesson 6 prove about texture detail?
Texture pixels can add material detail without adding more cubes or mesh geometry.
What is the danger of adding too many marks?
The prop becomes noisy when zoomed out, so the material reads worse instead of better.

The one new idea - clusters beat specks

A cluster is a small connected group of pixels that share a color or purpose. Beginner pixel art often fails because every detail is an isolated one-pixel speck. For tiny 3D textures, use clusters for shadows, highlights, and material marks. This keeps the texture readable when the model is small. [Lospec - Creating Pixel Art by cure]

Noisy specks

Many isolated pixels. It looks detailed up close, but messy at game size.

Readable clusters

Grouped pixels. The material has a clear shadow side, base color, and tiny highlight.

Beginner rule

For today's homework, every material gets only three colors: shadow, base, highlight. This is enough to practice pixel art without drowning in choices.

Settings tutorial - tiny texture, crisp brush

Blockbench texture work depends on both the texture size and the painting tool settings. The official docs describe texture resolution and painting directly on the model. Today we deliberately keep the resolution small. [Blockbench texture docs]

Do it with me

Use the Course Palette and keep the Pixel Art Texture Cheatsheet open.

  1. Pick one wooden prop

    Use the crate, chopping board, bread basket, counter, or another wooden prop you already made. Save a copy so today's texture practice does not overwrite a version you like.

  2. Choose the three-color wood ramp

    Use Dark Wood #7A4E24 for shadow, Wood #C68B45 for base, and Cream #F3E6C4 for tiny highlights.

  3. Create or confirm a small texture

    If Blockbench asks for texture size, choose 32 x 32. If your prop already has a texture, keep it and work on one visible wooden face. Do not upscale today.

  4. Fill the face with the base color

    Use Paint Bucket or Brush to make the chosen wooden face mostly Wood. This is your quiet starting surface.

  5. Paint one shadow cluster

    With Brush size 1, paint a connected Dark Wood cluster along one lower edge or side edge. Make it 3-6 pixels long, not a full outline around the whole face.

  6. Paint one highlight cluster

    Add a tiny Cream cluster near the opposite upper edge. Keep it small: 1-3 pixels is enough.

  7. Add two material marks

    Add two short Dark Wood wood-grain clusters. They should be connected marks, not random single-pixel dust.

  8. Run the thumbnail test

    Orbit out until the prop is small. Ask: "Can I still tell this is wood?" If the face buzzes, undo or erase half the marks.

  9. Capture two screenshots

    Take one close-up screenshot of the texture pixels or UV editor, and one clean render of the prop. These are your itch.io proof images.

Homework

Your assignment

Create and post a Pixel Texture Study #1: Wood.

  1. Apply the three-color wood ramp to one wooden prop face.
  2. Use at least one shadow cluster, one highlight cluster, and two wood-grain clusters.
  3. Delete any marks that fail the thumbnail test.
  4. Post two images to itch.io: a close-up texture view and the final prop render.
  5. Send me the itch.io link afterward, and I will critique the texture before the next lesson.
Do not do this yet

Skip dithering, anti-aliasing, and complex material rendering today. Those are real pixel-art topics, but they are not the first bottleneck for your Blockbench asset pack. First we need clean clusters and readable contrast.

Quick self-check

Say the answer first, then open the card.

What is a cluster?
A connected group of pixels that share a color or purpose, such as a shadow or highlight.
How many colors should today's wood study use?
Three: shadow, base, and highlight.
What should you do if the texture looks busy when zoomed out?
Erase details until the main material reads clearly again.
Why keep the texture small?
A small texture forces clear choices and helps the asset keep a deliberate pixel-art style.
